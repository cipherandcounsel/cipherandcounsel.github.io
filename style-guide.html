Editorial
Style Guide

Writing conventions for cybersecurity research, policy communication, and threat intelligence documentation

This guide defines the editorial standards that govern all written output produced under the Cipher & Counsel platform. It exists because good technical writing is not intuitive — it is systematic. Every convention documented here reflects a deliberate choice about how to close the gap between what security researchers know and what decision-makers can act on.

Scope: All Cipher & Counsel Publications
Audience: Author · Editor · Contributor
Version: 1.0 · Living Document
Foundation

Editorial Mission

Every document produced under Cipher & Counsel serves one purpose: to get the right information to the right person in the right format so they can make a better decision. Technical accuracy is not enough. A finding that cannot be understood by its intended reader has not yet done its job.

The editorial standard at Cipher & Counsel is clarity without condescension and depth without inaccessibility. We write for readers who are intelligent but not necessarily technical. We never talk down. We never obscure. We translate.

The Test for Every Sentence

Before publishing, ask: Does this sentence help the reader decide something, understand something, or do something? If the answer is no, the sentence does not belong in the document.

Voice and Tone

How Cipher & Counsel Sounds

The Cipher & Counsel voice is authoritative without being cold, precise without being stiff, and accessible without being casual. It sounds like a knowledgeable colleague who respects the reader's time.

01
Write to a specific person, not a general audience
Before writing, identify the primary reader and what decision they need to make. A CISO reading a threat brief needs to know risk and response priority. A SOC analyst needs to know what to look for and how to act. Write to that person. If the document serves multiple audiences, use tabbed sections or clearly labeled audience callouts.
02
Lead with the finding, not the methodology
The reader wants to know what you found before they want to know how you found it. State the key finding in the first sentence or the first paragraph. Save methodology for supporting sections. Exception: academic research publications where methodology is the primary audience concern.
03
Confidence language must match evidence quality
Use assessed, likely, probable for analytical judgments supported by strong evidence. Use possible, may, could for lower-confidence assessments. Never state an analytical judgment as fact. Attribution claims always carry a confidence qualifier: attributed with high confidence or assessed with moderate confidence.
04
Eliminate throat-clearing
Remove any sentence that restates what the document is about, thanks the reader for their time, or announces what you are about to say instead of saying it. Start with content. The reader knows they are reading a report. Do not tell them they are reading a report.
Voice

Active vs Passive Voice

Default to active voice. Active voice names the actor and the action directly. It is shorter, clearer, and more authoritative. Passive voice obscures accountability and inflates sentence length.

Use passive voice only when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately withheld. In threat intelligence, passive voice is appropriate when attribution is unconfirmed. In policy and process documentation, active voice is almost always correct.

Avoid
"Malicious macros were executed by the attacker after the document was opened by the user."
Preferred
"The attacker executed malicious macros after the user opened the document."
Avoid
"The endpoint was isolated and the threat was contained."
Preferred
"The SOC analyst isolated the endpoint and confirmed containment."
Passive — Correct Use
"The payload was delivered via a macro-laden document. Attribution has not been confirmed at time of publication."
Active — Confirmed Attribution
"Boggy Serpens delivered the payload via a macro-laden document consistent with prior campaign TTPs."
Language

Technical Terms and Abbreviations

01
Define on first use, then abbreviate
Write the full term followed by the abbreviation in parentheses on first use in every document. Use the abbreviation only thereafter. Do not assume the reader knows the abbreviation even if it appears common in the field. Example: tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs) on first reference, then TTPs throughout.
02
Audience determines technical depth
For analyst audiences: technique IDs (T1566.001), malware family names, and IOC formats are appropriate without definition. For executive audiences: replace technique IDs with plain-language descriptions. Replace T1566.001 Spearphishing Attachment with a malicious document delivered by email. The technical accuracy does not change. The language does.
03
Never use jargon as a substitute for explanation
If a term cannot be defined in one clear sentence, it is being used as jargon. Either define it or replace it. Terms like threat actor, adversary, implant, persistence mechanism, and lateral movement all require a brief definition the first time they appear in any document written for a mixed audience.
04
Capitalize proper nouns, not common nouns used as jargon
Capitalize named frameworks, standards, and tools: MITRE ATT&CK, NIST SP 800-61r3, Cortex XDR, ISO 22301. Do not capitalize generic terms used as technical shorthand: ransomware, backdoor, playbook, threat actor, lateral movement. Exception: named malware families are capitalized: BlackBeard, POWERSTATS, Nuso.
Punctuation

Punctuation Conventions

·
No em dashes in any publication
Em dashes create visual noise and are inconsistently rendered across platforms and screen readers. Replace with a period, a comma, or a restructured sentence. If an em dash is creating a parenthetical, use parentheses or a separate sentence instead.
·
Oxford comma is required
Always use the serial comma before the final item in a list of three or more. Threat classification, severity rating, and confidence score not threat classification, severity rating and confidence score. Ambiguity in lists undermines precision.
·
Use the interpunct as a separator in labels and metadata
The centered dot ( · ) is the Cipher & Counsel separator for navigation labels, metadata strings, and multi-item inline lists where a comma would create confusion. Do not use pipes (|), slashes (/), or hyphens (-) as separators in display text.
·
Periods inside quotation marks for American English conventions
Follow American English punctuation conventions in all publications. Periods and commas go inside closing quotation marks. Question marks and exclamation points go inside only when they are part of the quoted material.
Numbers and Data

Handling Numbers and Statistics

01
Spell out numbers one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
Exception: always use numerals for percentages (65%), version numbers (v18), confidence scores (94), and technical measurements regardless of value.
02
Every statistic requires a source
No statistic appears in a Cipher & Counsel publication without an inline attribution or a footnote citation. Unsourced statistics undermine credibility regardless of accuracy. Use the format: According to the Unit 42 2026 Global Incident Response Report, attackers moved from initial access to exfiltration in under one hour in the fastest observed incidents.
03
Contextualize percentages with the base they reference
Do not write 65% of incidents. Write 65% of the 750 major incidents Unit 42 investigated in 2025. A percentage without a base number is meaningless and creates the appearance of precision without delivering it.
Structure

Audience Frameworks

Every document begins with a declared audience. The audience determines the vocabulary, the level of technical detail, the format, and the length. The same research finding requires a different document for each audience listed below.

Audience Primary Question Format Technical Depth Max Length
Board / Executive What is at risk and what does it cost us? One-pager, KPI cards, plain narrative None. Business language only. 1 page
General Counsel / Legal What are our obligations and liabilities? Annotated analysis, compliance gap table Regulatory language. No technique IDs. 2 to 3 pages
Operations Leadership What do we do and in what order? Phased roadmap, checklist, priority table Process-oriented. Action-focused. 2 pages
SOC Analyst / IR Team What do I look for and how do I respond? Playbook, ATT&CK table, IOC list Full technical depth. Technique IDs required. No limit
General Cybersecurity Community What happened and why does it matter to me? Narrative blog post, accessible threat brief Light. Define all terms. Tell the story. 800 to 1200 words
Document Types

Standard Document Formats

Threat Intelligence Report

Structure: Executive Summary, Actor Overview, Campaign Timeline, MITRE ATT&CK Mapping, Malware Toolkit, Detection Guidance, Citations. Always dual-audience: executive brief and technical analysis in separate clearly labeled sections or tabs.

Incident Response Documentation

Four required documents per incident: Technical Investigation Report, Executive Briefing, Escalation Log, Lessons Learned. Each document is self-contained and readable without the others. The Lessons Learned is formatted for the broadest possible distribution.

Policy Research Brief

Structure: Abstract, Introduction, Methodology, Framework Analysis, Key Findings, Recommendations, Citations. Written in academic register with a science communication layer. Every dense regulatory finding must be followed by a plain-language translation.

API Reference

Structure: Overview, Base URL, Authentication, Rate Limiting, Endpoints (each with path parameters, query parameters, example request, example response, response fields), Error Reference, Code Examples, SIEM Integration Guide, Changelog, Citations. All code examples must be functional and include error handling.

SOC Playbook

Structure: Purpose, Threat Context, Prerequisites, Numbered Steps (each with full narrative, tool alignment, ATT&CK mapping, and output), Reference Tables, Escalation Matrix, Citations. Steps are numbered. Each step is independently actionable without reading the others.

Headings and Labels

Heading Conventions

H1
Document title only. One per document.
The H1 is the document name. It appears once at the top. It is not repeated, summarized, or paraphrased anywhere else in the document.
H2
Major section headings. Complete thought, not a label.
H2 headings should communicate meaning, not just category. Prefer "What the Investigation Revealed" over "Findings." Prefer "Five Actions for the Next 30 Days" over "Recommendations."
H3
Subsection headings. Specific and descriptive.
H3 headings identify specific content within a major section. They are short, specific, and do not repeat language from the H2 above them.
TAG
Section labels in monospace uppercase for navigation and metadata
Short uppercase labels in IBM Plex Mono are used for navigation anchors, document metadata, and section identifiers. They appear above H2 headings and are not part of the document's prose hierarchy. Examples: THREAT CONTEXT · EXECUTIVE SUMMARY · DOCUMENT 01 OF 04
Sources

Citation Format and Source Standards

All factual claims, statistics, IOCs, TTPs, and threat actor attributions require a traceable source. Unsourced claims are not published. When sources conflict, both are cited and the conflict is noted explicitly.

Inline Attribution Format

Use for statistics and key findings within prose
According to the Unit 42 2026 Global Incident Response Report, attackers moved from initial access to data exfiltration in under one hour in the fastest observed incidents.

Footnote Citation Format

Use in reference tables and citation sections
Author / Organization — Title of Publication · Publisher · URL · Date
Unit 42 — Boggy Serpens Threat Assessment · Palo Alto Networks · unit42.paloaltonetworks.com · March 2026
MITRE ATT&CK — MuddyWater Group Profile G0069 · attack.mitre.org/groups/G0069 · Updated October 2025 (v18)

Source Hierarchy

Prefer sources in this order: primary standards documentation (NIST, ISO, IETF RFC), peer-reviewed academic research, named threat intelligence vendor reports (Unit 42, Group-IB, Mandiant, CrowdStrike), government advisories (CISA, FBI, NCSC), then secondary analysis. Do not cite aggregator sites, forums, or unnamed sources.

IOC Handling

Indicator of Compromise Conventions

!
Defang all IOCs in TLP:WHITE publications
Replace dots in IP addresses and domains with bracketed dots to prevent accidental navigation or automated tooling from resolving malicious infrastructure. 198.51.100.42 becomes 198.51.100[.]42. malicious-c2.example.com becomes malicious-c2.example[.]com. Always note that IOCs are defanged and must be restored before use in detection tooling.
!
Every IOC requires source attribution
IOCs without traceable sources are not published. Cite the report, the date, and the organization that identified or confirmed the indicator. Note when an IOC appears in multiple sources as corroboration.
!
Note IOC age and reliability
IOCs decay. An IP address observed in August 2025 may be reassigned or decommissioned by the time of publication. Always include first-seen and last-seen timestamps where available. Flag IOCs older than 90 days as potentially stale.
